Constructing a Tully-Fisher relation from galaxies observed by the SAMI survey.

 $$\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$ $$\frac{G(M/L)L}{r} = v^2$$ $$\frac{G(M/L)L}{v^2} = r$$ $$I = \frac{L}{2\pi r^2}$$ $$\textrm{where L is the intrinsic luminosity of the galaxy,}$$ $$\textrm{r is the radius of the galaxy and}$$ $$\textrm{I is it's surface brightness.}$$ $$r = \{\frac{L}{2\pi I}\}^{1/2}$$ $$\textrm{substituting for r, we get}$$ $$\frac{G(M/L)L}{v^2} = \{\frac{L}{2\pi I}\}^{1/2}$$ $$\{G\frac{M}{L}\}^2 2\pi I L^2 = L v^4$$ $$kL \propto v^4$$ $$\textrm{where}$$ $$k = \{G\frac{M}{L}\}^2 2\pi I$$ Here we assume that I, the surface brightness of galaxies is constant for all type of galaxies and that the M/L ratio is also constant. In reality, the presence of low surface brightness galaxies and high surface brightness galaxies increases scatter in the Tully-Fisher relationship.

A major part of the ARIES training school is data analysis and as part of a team of 5, I was studying the polarization of light from a star forming region Stock 8 in the constellation Auriga. The data was collected using the ARIES Imaging Polarimeter instrument (AImPol) at the ARIES 1.04m telescope.

We measured the stokes parameters of multiple stars and estimated the overall polarization of light. We estimated the density of dust in the star forming region using the polarization and using standard stars. We also differentiated between field stars and foreground stars by studying the amount of polarization.
